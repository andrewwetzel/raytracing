Ionospheric Ray Tracer

🔀 Run Modes

Fan Sweep

Traces multiple rays across a range of elevation angles simultaneously. This produces a "fan" of rays departing the transmitter, showing how signals at different departure angles propagate through the ionosphere. Useful for visualizing overall propagation geometry and skip zones.

Target Location

Finds the specific elevation angle (or other parameter) needed to reach a target ground range. Uses bisection search to converge on the right launch conditions. Quick mode bisects a single variable; Advanced mode sweeps multiple parameter combinations and finds the best match for each.

📻 Transmitter Settings

Frequency (MHz)

The radio wave operating frequency. Higher frequencies penetrate deeper into the ionosphere before refracting. Frequencies above the Maximum Usable Frequency (MUF) will punch through the ionosphere entirely and escape into space. Typical HF range: 2–30 MHz.

Ray Mode (O / X)

The Earth's magnetic field splits radio waves into two characteristic modes:

  • Ordinary (O-mode) — electric field oscillates parallel to the geomagnetic field plane. Refracts at the plasma frequency.
  • Extraordinary (X-mode) — electric field oscillates perpendicular to B. Refracts at a different (usually higher) frequency due to magneto-ionic coupling.

📐 Fan Sweep Parameters

Elevation Angles (Min / Max / Step)

Controls the departure angles of the ray fan, measured from the horizon upward. Low angles (5–15°) produce long-range "skip" propagation. High angles (60–89°) go nearly vertical. The step size determines how many rays are traced in the fan.

🌐 Ionosphere Parameters

Critical Frequency foF2 (MHz)

The peak plasma frequency of the F2 layer — the densest part of the ionosphere. A vertically-incident wave at this frequency will just reflect. Oblique rays can reflect at higher frequencies (up to ~3× foF2). Typical daytime values: 5–12 MHz; nighttime: 2–6 MHz.

Peak Height hmF2 (km)

The altitude of maximum electron density in the F2 layer. Higher hmF2 generally means longer skip distances because rays refract in a wider arc. Typical values: 200–400 km. Varies with solar activity, season, and time of day.

Scale Height (km)

Controls how "thick" the ionospheric layer is — the vertical distance over which electron density drops by a factor of e (~2.718). Larger scale heights produce a broader, more gradual density profile. Smaller values create a sharper, thinner reflecting layer. Typical range: 50–150 km.

Gyrofrequency fH (MHz)

The electron cyclotron frequency in the Earth's magnetic field — the frequency at which free electrons spiral around geomagnetic field lines. Determines the strength of magneto-ionic splitting between O and X modes. Typical surface value: 0.8–1.4 MHz; decreases with altitude.

🧪 Physics Models

Electron Density Models

  • Chapman — Classic single-layer Chapman profile. Good general-purpose model with analytic gradients.
  • ELECT1 — Tabulated multi-layer profile from the original Fortran code with interpolated gradients.
  • Linear — Simple piecewise-linear density increasing to hmF2 then decreasing. Useful as a baseline.
  • Quasi-Parabolic — Parabolic density profile centered on hmF2. Smooth gradients, analytic ray paths.
  • Var Chapman — Chapman layer with altitude-varying scale height for more realistic profiles.
  • Dual Chapman — Two superimposed Chapman layers (E + F2 regions) for realistic multi-layer refraction.

Magnetic Field Models

  • Dipole — Centered geomagnetic dipole. Simple but captures the essential field geometry.
  • Constant — Uniform field direction and magnitude everywhere. Useful for isolating magneto-ionic effects.
  • Cubic — Higher-order polynomial field model with more realistic spatial variation.
  • IGRF-14 — International Geomagnetic Reference Field (degree 13). The most accurate real-world model, using spherical harmonic coefficients valid 1900–2030.

Refractive Index Models

  • Full AH — Complete Appleton-Hartree formula with magnetic field and collisions. Most physically accurate.
  • No Field/Col — Ignores both magnetic field and collisions. Simple X = (fp/f)² refraction only.
  • No Field — Includes collisions but ignores the magnetic field. No O/X mode splitting.
  • No Collisions — Includes magnetic field but ignores electron-neutral collisions. No absorption.

Perturbation Models

  • None — Undisturbed ionosphere.
  • Torus — Localized toroidal density enhancement, simulating a plasma bubble or TID.
  • Trough — Mid-latitude electron density depletion (density trough).
  • Shock — Propagating density discontinuity, modeling a shockwave disturbance.
  • Bulge — Broad density enhancement, simulating the equatorial anomaly or storm-enhanced density.
  • Exp — Exponential density perturbation profile.

🚀 Quick Start

1
Choose a mode

Select Fan Sweep to visualize many rays at once, or Target Location to find how to reach a specific ground range.

2
Set the frequency

Adjust the transmitter frequency. Start with 10 MHz for a typical HF scenario. Lower frequencies reflect more easily; higher frequencies reach farther or escape.

3
Configure the ionosphere

Adjust foF2 (critical frequency), hmF2 (layer height), and Scale Height to simulate different ionospheric conditions. Defaults represent a typical daytime mid-latitude ionosphere.

4
Trace the rays

Click ▶ Trace Rays (Fan mode) or 🎯 Find Path (Target mode). Rays will be computed instantly in your browser via WebAssembly.

5
Explore the map

Click and drag to pan. Scroll wheel to zoom. Hover over any ray for detailed info. Double-click to reset the view.

🔬 Comparing Configurations

1
Enable multiple options

Check multiple boxes in any group (modes, density models, magnetic models, etc.). Each combination becomes a separate trace group with its own color.

2
Use sweep ranges

Click the 🔀 button next to any numeric parameter to enable a sweep range. Set min, max, and step to generate multiple traces across that parameter space.

3
Watch the combo counter

The badge on the Trace button shows how many configurations will be run. Keep it under 50 for best performance.

🎯 Target Mode Tips

1
Set a target range

Enter the desired ground range in km. The map will auto-zoom to show the transmitter and target. A pink marker shows the target location on the globe.

2
Choose a search variable

In Quick mode, pick which parameter to bisect (elevation, frequency, foF2, etc.). The search will find the value that lands a ray closest to your target.

3
Use Advanced for multi-parameter exploration

Advanced mode sweeps multiple ionospheric parameters and bisects elevation for each combo, ranking all results by accuracy. Great for finding optimal propagation conditions.

💾 Exporting Results

After a fan trace, export your rays as KML (for Google Earth) or GeoJSON (for GIS tools). Exports include 3D coordinates with altitude for each ray point.
